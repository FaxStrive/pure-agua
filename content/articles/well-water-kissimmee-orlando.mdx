# Well Water in Kissimmee and Orlando: Problems, Testing, and Treatment for Central Florida

<time dateTime="2026-04-15">Last updated: April 2026</time>

Central Florida has a substantial population of private well owners. In rural areas of Osceola County south and west of Kissimmee, large portions of Polk County, rural sections of Orange County, and communities scattered throughout the surrounding region, many homeowners draw their water from private wells rather than municipal systems.

Well water in Central Florida comes with a fundamentally different set of challenges than city water. When you're on a private well, there's no utility doing daily treatment and regulatory compliance testing. Your well is your water supply — you own it, you're responsible for it, and what comes out of it depends entirely on local geology and what you've done to treat it.

This guide covers the specific problems that Central Florida well owners face, why they differ from municipal water issues, how to test properly, and what treatment solutions are appropriate for the actual conditions you'll find in Osceola, Orange, and Polk County groundwater.

## Understanding Central Florida Well Water Sources

Most private wells in Central Florida draw from one of two aquifer zones:

**Surficial Aquifer System:** The shallowest groundwater zone, typically within 100 feet of the surface. In most of Central Florida, the surficial aquifer is not a primary water supply source for homes — it tends to produce lower yields and is more vulnerable to surface contamination than deeper sources. Some older rural homes in the area have shallow wells from the surficial aquifer.

**Intermediate Aquifer System:** A middle zone present in some parts of Central Florida, particularly in Polk County. The Intermediate System includes the Hawthorn Formation, which contains pockets of usable water but also naturally occurring minerals including phosphate (the Bone Valley phosphate region covers much of Polk County) that can affect water quality.

**Upper Floridan Aquifer:** The vast majority of productive private wells in Central Florida are drilled to the Upper Floridan Aquifer — the same formation that municipal utilities pump from, but without the benefit of centralized treatment before delivery to your tap.

The Floridan Aquifer in Osceola County, rural Orange County, and Polk County has distinct characteristics:

**Higher mineral content than municipal finished water.** Municipal utilities blend aquifer water, sometimes treat for hardness or minerals, and deliver finished water. Your private well delivers raw aquifer water. Hardness in Osceola County Floridan Aquifer wells commonly ranges 20–40 GPG — substantially harder than the 12–22 GPG KUA municipal customers experience after utility treatment.

**Anaerobic conditions.** Deep aquifer water is oxygen-depleted (anaerobic). This is important because it keeps iron and manganese in their dissolved forms (invisible in water) and supports the sulfate-reducing bacteria that produce hydrogen sulfide.

**No disinfection residual.** Municipal water arrives at your tap with a chloramine or chlorine residual that suppresses bacterial growth in your household plumbing. Your well water has no such protection — what's in the aquifer and what gets into your system reaches your tap directly.

## Problem 1: Iron and Manganese Staining

Iron is the most universally reported problem among Central Florida well owners. In the Floridan Aquifer's anaerobic environment, iron exists in its dissolved ferrous (Fe²⁺) form — the water looks completely clear when it comes out of the tap.

Then it hits oxygen. In the toilet tank, a stainless steel sink, laundry, or even just sitting in a glass, dissolved ferrous iron oxidizes to ferric iron (Fe³⁺) and precipitates as rust-colored particles. The result is the orange-red staining that characterizes untreated well water homes throughout Osceola and Polk Counties.

Iron concentrations in Central Florida wells vary considerably:
- Low: 0.3–1 mg/L (some staining, EPA secondary standard is 0.3 mg/L)
- Moderate: 1–5 mg/L (significant staining on all fixtures)
- High: 5–20 mg/L (severe staining, orange water visible at the tap)
- Very high: 20+ mg/L (water is visibly orange/brown, staining is immediate and severe)

Wells in the Osceola County area frequently test at 2–8 mg/L of dissolved iron — the moderate to high range. At these levels, staining of toilets, tubs, sinks, and laundry occurs rapidly. Concrete driveways and irrigation systems show visible rust staining. Irrigation heads clog with iron deposits.

**Manganese** often accompanies iron in Central Florida groundwater. Manganese causes black staining (as opposed to iron's orange-red) and has a lower aesthetic threshold — above 0.05 mg/L, manganese causes black staining on fixtures and dark specks in water. The EPA's secondary standard for manganese is 0.05 mg/L, and the EPA has issued a health advisory suggesting that manganese above 0.1 mg/L warrants treatment, particularly for infant formula preparation, due to neurological development concerns.

**Treatment:** The correct treatment depends on iron and manganese concentration and form:
- Dissolved iron + manganese at moderate levels: Oxidizing filter using catalytic iron removal media (greensand plus, Birm, or similar). The filter oxidizes dissolved iron and manganese to particle form and filters them out.
- High iron concentrations: Chlorine injection (chemical oxidation) before filtration provides more aggressive and consistent oxidation, followed by backwashing catalytic filter.
- Iron bacteria (orange slime in toilet tanks, well casing): These bacteria form a biofilm that shields iron from standard chemical treatment. Shock chlorination of the well followed by ongoing treatment is required.
- Iron + hardness: A combination system addressing both issues is usually more cost-effective than separate units.

## Problem 2: Hydrogen Sulfide — The Rotten-Egg Smell

Hydrogen sulfide is the second most common well water complaint in Central Florida. The rotten-egg odor associated with H₂S is detectable at extremely low concentrations — human olfactory sensitivity to H₂S is around 0.0005 mg/L. By the time you smell it, you're definitely dealing with it.

H₂S in Floridan Aquifer water is produced by sulfate-reducing bacteria (SRB) — microorganisms that live in the anaerobic aquifer environment and metabolize sulfate (naturally abundant in Florida groundwater) to produce H₂S as a byproduct. The SRBs are in the aquifer, in biofilms on the well casing and pump, and potentially throughout your pressure system.

At concentrations found in residential wells (typically 0.05–3 mg/L), hydrogen sulfide is an aesthetic problem — not a direct health hazard at these levels — but it is corrosive to metal plumbing and pump components. The anode rod in your water heater, which protects the tank from corrosion, is consumed far faster in H₂S water. Pumps and pressure tanks in high-H₂S wells require more frequent maintenance and replacement.

**Treatment options:**
- **Aeration:** H₂S is a dissolved gas with limited water solubility. Aeration (introducing air into the water) causes H₂S to off-gas, dramatically reducing concentrations. Simple retention/aeration tanks can reduce H₂S by 70–90% for mild to moderate concentrations.
- **Catalytic media filtration:** Carbon-based media can oxidize and adsorb H₂S, but regenerates quickly in high-H₂S water. Works best for mild concentrations (<0.5 mg/L) as a polishing step.
- **Chlorine injection:** Chlorine oxidizes H₂S to elemental sulfur and sulfate, eliminating the odor completely. Followed by catalytic filtration to remove sulfur particles. This approach also provides bacterial control. Appropriate for moderate to high H₂S concentrations.
- **Continuous chlorination + carbon:** Some systems use chlorine injection for oxidation, then carbon filtration to remove both elemental sulfur and residual chlorine, delivering clean, odor-free water with no chemical taste.

Do not try to solve H₂S with carbon filtration alone — carbon beds used for H₂S removal saturate rapidly (H₂S at aquifer concentrations consumes carbon capacity quickly) and require frequent, expensive media replacement without addressing the source. Aeration or oxidation is the right first step.

## Problem 3: Bacteria — The Invisible Health Risk

Municipal water arrives with chlorination providing ongoing bacterial control. Your private well delivers raw groundwater — and the Floridan Aquifer, while generally a clean geological environment, is not sterile.

More importantly, the pathway from aquifer to tap includes your well casing, submersible pump, pressure tank, and household plumbing — all surfaces where bacteria can colonize and persist even if the aquifer water itself is clean. Biofilm in well components is the source of most bacterial detections in otherwise-protected Florida wells.

**E. coli and total coliform:** The EPA's microbiological standard is zero detectable coliform bacteria per 100 mL sample and zero E. coli. Any detection of coliform bacteria in a well indicates either aquifer contamination, well construction issues that allow surface water introduction, or (most commonly) biofilm in the well system.

In Central Florida's context:
- Rural Osceola County has numerous septic systems — standard in areas not on municipal sewage. Septic systems, properly maintained, should not contaminate groundwater. But failed or undersized systems can introduce coliform bacteria to shallow groundwater, and in areas of high-density rural development with both shallow wells and septic systems, there's potential for interaction.
- Flooding events — not uncommon during Florida's hurricane season and wet-season storm events — can introduce surface water and bacterial contamination into vulnerable wells.
- Old, poorly sealed wells (common on properties more than 20–30 years old) may have casing seal failures that allow surface water intrusion.

**Testing and response:**
EPA recommends annual bacterial testing for all private wells. Any confirmed coliform detection requires:
1. Shock chlorination of the well (disinfecting the well casing and system)
2. Retesting to confirm clearance
3. Investigation of the contamination source if coliform recurs

If bacterial contamination is chronic or recurrent, a UV disinfection system is the recommended continuous treatment. UV at 254nm delivers a lethal dose of ultraviolet radiation to bacteria, viruses, and protozoa (including Giardia and Cryptosporidium) without adding any chemicals to the water and without affecting water taste.

UV systems require clear water (iron, turbidity, and tannins reduce UV penetration) — typically the UV unit is installed after iron and filtration treatment.

## Problem 4: High Hardness Without Utility Softening

Municipal utilities deliver water at hardness levels that, while hard by national standards, represent the Floridan Aquifer water after it has potentially been blended with softer surface water or partially treated. Your private well delivers raw aquifer water — and in Osceola County, that means 20–40 GPG hardness is common.

This is 2–4 times higher than the "very hard" threshold. The scale formation rate in an untreated home at 30 GPG is devastating. Water heaters at this hardness level may fail in 4–6 years. Tankless units clog within 1–2 years. Scale deposits on shower doors, faucets, and fixtures are thick, visible, and tenacious.

For well water at 20+ GPG, a properly sized water softener is not an optional upgrade — it's a necessity for maintaining the functionality of home plumbing and appliances. ## Well Water vs. City Water: Making the Comparison

**Cost:** City water has no upfront infrastructure cost but carries monthly usage fees — typically $30–$80/month for a Central Florida household, or $360–$960/year. Over 20 years, that's $7,200–$19,200 in water bills. A private well has upfront drilling costs ($3,000–$8,000 for a new residential well in Central Florida) but no monthly usage fees once installed. Electricity for the pump is typically $5–$15/month. Well water is substantially cheaper over any horizon beyond 5–7 years.

**Treatment requirements:** City water is pre-treated but still contains chloramines, disinfection byproducts, and utility-level hardness. Well water is untreated but avoids chlorination altogether. Both require home treatment for optimal quality — well water requires more comprehensive treatment (iron, bacteria, hardness, H₂S) while city water requires chloramine removal and hardness treatment.

**Control:** Well water gives you complete control over your water supply and its treatment. You're not subject to utility rate increases, water use restrictions, or distribution system events. You're also entirely responsible for your system's maintenance and testing.

## Testing Recommendations for Central Florida Well Owners

**At minimum (EPA recommendation): Annual testing**
- Total coliform and E. coli (bacteria)
- Nitrates (common in agricultural areas of Osceola and Polk Counties)
- pH

**Complete well water profile (recommended at installation and every 2–3 years):**
- Full bacteria panel (coliform, E. coli)
- Nitrates and nitrites
- pH
- Total hardness
- Iron (dissolved ferrous and total)
- Manganese
- Hydrogen sulfide
- Total Dissolved Solids (TDS)
- Arsenic (naturally occurring in Floridan Aquifer zones)
- Fluoride
- Tannins (if water has any color)

**After flooding:** Immediately test for bacteria and conduct visual inspection of well cap and casing.

**If you're on or near agricultural land:** Add pesticides/herbicides panel and nitrate levels.
