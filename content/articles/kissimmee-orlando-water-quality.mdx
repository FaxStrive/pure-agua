# Kissimmee and Orlando Water Quality: OUC, KUA, and What Central Florida Residents Need to Know

<time dateTime="2026-04-15">Last updated: April 2026</time>

Central Florida's water quality is shaped by one dominant geological reality: nearly all of the region's groundwater comes from the Floridan Aquifer, one of the most productive aquifer systems in the world and one of the most mineral-rich. Whether you're in Kissimmee, Orlando, Celebration, Ocoee, or any of the dozens of communities across Orange and Osceola Counties, your municipal water has been underground, dissolving limestone, for potentially thousands of years before it reaches your tap.

Understanding what that means — and how the two primary utilities serving Central Florida handle it — is essential context for every homeowner making decisions about water treatment.

## The Floridan Aquifer: Central Florida's Water Foundation

The Upper Floridan Aquifer is a massive, confined limestone aquifer system extending across virtually all of Florida and into Georgia, Alabama, and South Carolina. In Central Florida, the aquifer lies beneath a confining layer of clay at depths typically ranging from 200–1,000 feet, depending on location.

For the Kissimmee-Orlando metro, the aquifer is the primary water source for both municipal utilities and private wells. As precipitation falls and percolates through the soil and into the aquifer recharge zones (primarily in the Lake Wales Ridge area of Central Florida), it picks up calcium carbonate and magnesium carbonate from the limestone. By the time that water has traveled through the aquifer to Central Florida utility wellfields, it's carrying significant mineral loads.

This is why Central Florida has hard water. It's not a treatment failure or a municipal choice — it's the direct result of the region's geology.

## Orlando Utilities Commission (OUC) Water Quality

OUC — the Orlando Utilities Commission — provides water service to the City of Orlando and portions of Orange County. As one of the Southeast's larger public utilities, OUC serves approximately 250,000 customer accounts.

OUC draws water primarily from the Upper Floridan Aquifer through a network of production wells in Orange County. The utility also participates in regional water supply diversification programs that include treated surface water and alternative water sources, blending these with Floridan Aquifer water at treatment facilities before distribution.

**Hardness:** OUC's finished water typically measures 9–14 grains per gallon (GPG) in most service areas. Some areas on the periphery of OUC's service territory or during periods of higher aquifer reliance may see hardness at the higher end of this range. The OUC annual Consumer Confidence Report (CCR) publishes average hardness; your specific address may vary slightly based on distribution zone.

At 9–14 GPG, OUC water is solidly in the "hard" to "very hard" range. The Water Quality Association defines water above 10.5 GPG as "very hard" — most OUC customers are at or above this threshold. This hardness level causes:
- Scale accumulation in water heaters (reducing efficiency 25–40%, per DOE research)
- Reduced appliance lifespan (dishwashers, washing machines, tankless water heaters)
- White deposits on fixtures, showerheads, and glassware
- Reduced effectiveness of soaps, shampoos, and detergents

**Chloramination:** OUC uses chloramines — not free chlorine — as its primary distribution disinfectant. This is a critical fact that most Orlando homeowners don't know, and it has significant implications for filtration. Standard activated carbon filters are ineffective at removing chloramines in the contact times available in residential filtration systems. Catalytic carbon is required. This applies to every OUC customer in the Orlando area.

**Disinfection byproducts:** OUC's CCR reports total trihalomethanes (TTHMs) and haloacetic acids (HAAs5) within EPA MCLs. However, the EWG's Tap Water Database shows that OUC's THM levels exceed EWG health guidelines at certain times of year — particularly summer months when warmer source water and higher demand require more aggressive disinfection treatment. TTHMs at concentrations above EWG's guideline are associated with increased bladder cancer risk with long-term exposure.

**PFAS:** OUC has been proactive in PFAS monitoring and reports PFAS levels consistently below the EPA's 2024 MCLs of 4 parts per trillion for PFOA and PFOS. However, EWG's health guideline of 1 ppt combined means that some detections still exceed the more stringent health-based threshold. Reverse osmosis provides greater than 95% reduction for PFAS as a precautionary measure.

## Kissimmee Utility Authority (KUA) Water Quality

KUA — the Kissimmee Utility Authority — is an independent community-owned utility serving Kissimmee and portions of Osceola County. While smaller than OUC, KUA serves a fast-growing service territory that includes Kissimmee proper, portions of Celebration, and surrounding communities.

KUA draws from the Upper Floridan Aquifer. Osceola County's section of the aquifer is notably mineral-rich — the geology in this part of Central Florida produces water with higher hardness than much of the OUC service territory.

**Hardness:** KUA finished water typically measures 12–22 GPG across its service area. This is among the harder municipal water in the Central Florida region. The distinctive rotten-egg odor that many Kissimmee residents notice — particularly when water is warm — is hydrogen sulfide naturally present in Osceola County aquifer water. KUA's treatment process reduces but does not always fully eliminate hydrogen sulfide at aesthetic levels.

At 12–22 GPG, Kissimmee's hard water problem is more severe than Orlando's. Scale formation is rapid and visible. Water heaters in Kissimmee without softening treatment frequently fail 4–6 years early. Tankless water heater manufacturers' warranties are often voided by hard water damage — and Osceola County's hardness levels are far above the threshold at which most manufacturers recommend softening.

**Chloramination:** KUA, like OUC, uses chloramines. The same catalytic carbon requirement applies to Kissimmee customers. Standard carbon filters from hardware stores and big-box retailers will not meaningfully reduce chloramine levels in KUA water.

**Total Dissolved Solids (TDS):** KUA water typically has higher TDS than OUC — often 300–500 mg/L in finished water. The higher TDS reflects the greater mineral load from Osceola County's aquifer geology. TDS at this level is detectable in taste and contributes to scale formation on heat-transfer surfaces.

## Orange County Utilities: Between OUC and Kissimmee

Orange County Utilities (OCU) serves unincorporated Orange County — communities outside the City of Orlando and municipal boundaries. OCU has made significant investments in alternative water sources, including a system of reclaimed water use and surface water treatment that supplements Floridan Aquifer groundwater.

OCU's Southern Water Treatment Plant uses reverse osmosis to treat deep aquifer water before blending it with other sources. This blending approach results in finished water with lower average hardness than KUA and comparable to or slightly lower than OUC — typically 8–14 GPG across OCU's service area — but the water chemistry from blended sources is more complex.

OCU's CCR shows PFAS monitoring results and TTHM levels that, while meeting EPA standards, warrant attention from customers using drinking water for infants, pregnant women, or immunocompromised family members.

## What the Water Quality Data Means for Your Home

Regardless of which Central Florida utility serves you, several water quality realities are universal:

**You have hard water.** Even at the low end of Central Florida's range — 9 GPG — water benefits from softening for appliance protection. At 15–22 GPG (common in Kissimmee and parts of Orange County), softening is essential. Without treatment, expect accelerated appliance failure, elevated energy costs, and constant scale management.

**Your water uses chloramines.** Both OUC and KUA chloraminate. Standard filters from retail stores don't address chloramines. If you have a refrigerator filter, pitcher filter, or under-sink carbon unit, it may be doing essentially nothing for the primary disinfectant in your water.

**Disinfection byproducts are present.** TTHMs and HAAs at levels that meet EPA limits but exceed EWG health guidelines are a consistent finding in Central Florida CCR data. These are most significant for long-term drinking water exposure, and reverse osmosis provides >95% reduction.

**Well water adds complexity.** Homeowners in rural Osceola County, south Orange County, and surrounding areas on private wells may face additional challenges beyond municipal customers — higher iron, stronger H₂S, bacteria risk, and hardness that can exceed municipal levels significantly.
