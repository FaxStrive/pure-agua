# The Floridan Aquifer and Hard Water in Central Florida: What It Costs You and How to Fix It

<time dateTime="2026-04-15">Last updated: April 2026</time>

Central Florida has hard water. This isn't a surprise to anyone who has lived here for more than a few months — the white crust around your faucets, the spots on your dishes, the showerhead that's slowly becoming a trickle — these are the visible signatures of water that carries more dissolved minerals than it should for residential use.

But understanding why Central Florida water is hard, and what it's actually costing you in appliance damage, energy bills, and ongoing soap waste, changes how you think about water softening — from a convenience upgrade to a practical investment with a calculable return.

## The Floridan Aquifer: A Geological Overview

The Floridan Aquifer System is one of the most extensive and productive aquifer systems in the world. It underlies approximately 100,000 square miles across Florida, Georgia, Alabama, and South Carolina. In Florida, the Upper Floridan Aquifer is the primary water source for much of the state.

The aquifer is not a buried lake or river — it's a thick sequence of highly porous limestone and dolomite rock, often hundreds to thousands of feet thick, saturated with water. The porosity and permeability of this rock is what makes it productive (water flows through it easily) and what makes it the source of Central Florida's hard water problem.

**Limestone is calcium carbonate.** As water percolates into the aquifer through recharge zones — primarily the highlands of the Lake Wales Ridge in Central Florida, where sandy soils allow rapid infiltration — it picks up carbon dioxide from decomposing organic matter in the soil. This CO₂ combines with water to form carbonic acid, which then dissolves the calcium carbonate limestone. The reaction: CO₂ + H₂O + CaCO₃ → Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻. The calcium ions that result are what makes your water hard.

By the time Floridan Aquifer water reaches Central Florida utility wellfields or your private well, it may have spent years or decades dissolving limestone. The result is water loaded with calcium — and in some geological zones, magnesium from dolomite formations — at concentrations that translate directly into the scale problems you see in your home.

**Central Florida's aquifer geography amplifies the problem.** The Kissimmee area, southern Orange County, and Osceola County sit over sections of the Floridan Aquifer where the limestone is particularly reactive and the water has more residence time to dissolve minerals. This is why Kissimmee Utility Authority water tests 12–22 GPG while some other Florida regions with shallower or less reactive aquifer sections see lower hardness.

## Measuring Hardness: What 12-22 GPG Actually Means

Water hardness is measured in grains per gallon (GPG) or milligrams per liter (mg/L). One grain per gallon equals 17.1 mg/L. Classification:

| Hardness Level | GPG | Common Description |
|---|---|---|
| Soft | 0–3.5 | No softening needed |
| Moderately hard | 3.5–7 | Softening beneficial |
| Hard | 7–10.5 | Softening recommended |
| Very hard | 10.5+ | Softening strongly recommended |

Kissimmee's 12–22 GPG range is 14–110% above the "very hard" threshold. Orlando (OUC) at 9–14 GPG is at or above very hard in most service areas. Orange County Utilities at 8–14 GPG similarly exceeds the recommendation threshold throughout most of its territory.

What does 15 GPG mean physically? Every gallon of water flowing through your home contains approximately 257 milligrams of dissolved calcium and magnesium. Your household uses hundreds to thousands of gallons per day. That's hundreds of grams of mineral being deposited throughout your plumbing, appliances, and water-using fixtures every day — day after day, year after year.

## How Hard Water Damages Appliances: The Specific Mechanisms

Scale is not just cosmetic. It's a thermally insulating, flow-restricting, mechanically abrasive compound that causes real damage to your home's most expensive systems.

### Water Heaters

The water heater is where hard water damage is most severe and most expensive. There are two distinct damage mechanisms:

**Heating element scale:** In electric water heaters, the heating element is in direct contact with the water. Calcium carbonate precipitates out of solution onto the element surface as water is heated (the solubility of calcium carbonate decreases at higher temperatures — the opposite of most dissolved compounds). A layer of scale 1/8 inch thick on a heating element reduces heat transfer efficiency by approximately 20%. At 1/4 inch — a common thickness in homes without softeners after several years — efficiency loss exceeds 40%, according to New Mexico State University research cited by the U.S. Department of Energy.

**Sediment accumulation:** In gas and electric tank water heaters, calcium carbonate precipitates as sediment that settles to the bottom of the tank where the burner (or lower heating element) is located. This sediment layer insulates the water from heat, requiring the burner to run longer and hotter. The result is increased energy consumption, accelerated burner wear, and a characteristic popping/rumbling sound when the water heater runs — the sound of steam forming under the sediment layer.

**Lifespan impact:** A tank water heater rated for 12–15 years in soft water conditions typically fails at 7–9 years in Kissimmee's 15–22 GPG water. The sediment-insulated burner runs hotter than designed, eventually causing element failure or cracking the tank's glass lining. Replacement cost: $800–$1,800 installed in the Central Florida area.

**Tankless water heaters at even greater risk:** The compact heat exchanger in tankless units — the component that provides on-demand heating — is highly sensitive to scale. At Central Florida hardness levels, heat exchanger scale accumulates within 1–2 years of installation. Manufacturers like Rinnai, Navien, and Rheem explicitly require water softening for warranty coverage in hard water areas (typically defined as areas above 8–11 GPG, depending on manufacturer). Descaling service for a tankless unit runs $150–$400, recommended annually in hard water conditions. Early heat exchanger replacement: $500–$900.

### Dishwashers

**Spray arm clogging:** Dishwasher spray arm jets are 1.5–2.5mm in diameter. Scale deposits narrow these jets over time, reducing spray pressure and cleaning effectiveness. Once spray arm jets are more than 50% occluded, cleaning performance drops measurably.

**Interior etching and scale:** Scale deposits on the dishwasher's interior stainless steel tub, heating element, and door gasket. The heating element — which boosts water temperature during the wash cycle and heats the air during drying — is subject to the same scale accumulation as water heater elements.

**Glassware:** Hot water, hard minerals, and dishwasher detergent create a chemically aggressive environment that permanently etches glass over years of exposure. The etching is physical damage to the glass surface — not removable with vinegar or cleaning products.

**Detergent overconsumption:** The Water Quality Research Foundation has quantified the detergent waste from hard water. Households in 17 GPG water — close to Kissimmee's range — use approximately 70% more dishwasher detergent than soft water households to achieve comparable cleaning results. At $8–$15 per unit of detergent, this adds up to $100–$200 in wasted dishwasher detergent annually.

### Washing Machines

**Fabric damage:** Calcium and magnesium deposits in washed fabrics make them stiffer, rougher, and more prone to fiber breakdown. Colors fade faster in hard water — the minerals interfere with dye bonding and create an abrasive environment that damages fabric during agitation. White fabrics develop a gray or yellow cast from mineral buildup. WQRF research found that fabrics washed in soft water retained their appearance significantly longer than those washed in hard water.

**Internal components:** Scale accumulates on the pump impeller, inlet valve screens, drum bearings, and (in front-loaders with internal heaters) the heating element. This reduces mechanical efficiency and causes premature failure of components that are expensive to replace.

**Laundry detergent waste:** Studies show Central Florida-hardness households use 50–70% more laundry detergent to overcome hard water's effect on soap performance. Calcium and magnesium ions bind to soap molecules, preventing them from forming the micelles that lift and suspend dirt. The excess detergent is also not fully rinsed from fabrics — it accumulates in fibers and contributes to the rough texture of laundry washed in hard water.

### Plumbing and Fixtures

Scale accumulates inside pipes, reducing internal diameter over time. In homes with galvanized steel pipes — common in Central Florida homes built before 1985 — scale deposits can reduce pipe diameter by 30–50% over 10–15 years of exposure to 15+ GPG water. Reduced flow forces higher pump pressure, increases wear on valves and pressure regulators, and eventually requires repiping.

Fixture cartridges, ball valves, and pressure reducing valves are all subject to scale-induced failure. Scale on valve seats prevents tight shut-off, and scale particles circulating in water damage valve and faucet cartridges. In hard water areas, faucet cartridge replacement is a common repair — $100–$250 per faucet for plumber-installed cartridges.

## Water Softener Options for Central Florida

Traditional ion exchange water softeners remain the most effective and most proven technology for hard water at Central Florida's hardness levels. The process exchanges calcium and magnesium ions for sodium ions using a resin bed, regenerating periodically with salt (sodium chloride or potassium chloride).

Properly sized for your home's water usage and hardness level, a salt-based softener delivers water at less than 1 GPG hardness — essentially zero from a scale perspective — throughout your home. Every appliance, every fixture, every shower, and every load of laundry benefits.

Salt-free conditioners (also marketed as "descalers") work by altering the crystal form of calcium carbonate so it doesn't adhere to surfaces as readily. They're a useful option for households with sodium dietary restrictions or light environmental preferences, but they don't remove calcium and magnesium from water — they only affect how the minerals deposit. For severe hardness like Kissimmee's 22 GPG, pure salt-free conditioning typically isn't sufficient for comprehensive appliance protection.
